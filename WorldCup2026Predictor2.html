01 — Where we left off

A good model,
with rough edges

The first instalment adapted Andrew Gelman's 2014 hierarchical model to the 48-team 2026 World Cup. It worked. But it leaned heavily on the FIFA rankings, treated draws as an afterthought, and — as it turned out — had a quiet bug in its data.

The baseline was elegant: model the score difference between two teams as a Student-t draw centred on the gap in their latent abilities, with each ability pulled toward a FIFA-ranking prior. Win, draw and loss came from slicing the predicted difference at ±0.5 goals. It trained on 110 games — the 2022 World Cup plus 2026 qualifiers — and produced sensible numbers for all 72 group fixtures.

Yet three things nagged. With only 110 games the model leaned hard on the prior (the ranking weight b ≈ 1.24). Nine qualified teams had no usable match data at all. And the ±0.5 threshold systematically under-counted draws — the most common result in tournament football.

What follows is the story of closing each gap, roughly in the order it happened: a data fix, a real prior, more and better games, a second model built for goals, a full tournament simulation, and finally the maths of the leaderboard the predictions feed into.

02 — A typo in the data

The team that
vanished

The first improvement cost one line of code and exposed a habit worth keeping. The notebook listed one Group E side as "Curacoa". The match database — the community-maintained martj42/international_results set — spells it "Curaçao", with the cedilla.

Because the name never matched, Curaçao's lone competitive result (a 5–1 loss to Haiti in qualifying) was silently dropped, and the team was quietly relegated to prior-only status — its rating driven entirely by the ranking, with no data update. Nothing errored. The model just ran with a phantom.

"Nothing crashed, and that was the danger. A string that doesn't match doesn't raise an exception — it just quietly removes a team from the evidence."

Fixing the spelling lifted the training set from 109 to 110 games and gave Curaçao a real, if unflattering, data point. A trivial change — but it set the tone for everything after: the model is only ever as good as the rows that survive the filters.

03 — Real rankings, real measuring sticks

Stop throwing
away the data

Two changes here, and together they were the biggest single jump. First, a real FIFA ranking. Then a rule that stopped discarding the most informative games in the dataset.

The original "April 2025" ranking was a hand-curated table — close to reality, but quietly fictional, with only a handful of gaps in the top 50. We replaced it wholesale with the actual FIFA/Coca-Cola World Ranking of 3 April 2025, pulled straight from FIFA's own JSON API, for every team in the model.

The deeper problem was the training filter. The baseline kept only games between two qualified teams — safe, but wasteful. Norway and Scotland's most informative qualifiers were against Italy. Several South American sides played Peru, Chile and Venezuela. None of those opponents qualified, so all those games were thrown away — which is exactly why nine teams had nothing to learn from.

The new rule keeps a game when both sides are in scope: a 2026 qualifier or a side ranked in the FIFA top 50. The strong non-qualifiers join the model purely as "measuring sticks" — we never predict their games, but their results pin down the qualifiers who faced them. Fourteen of them entered, expanding the model to a 62-team universe.

The threshold keeps the original safeguard intact: a 10–0 over San Marino (ranked ~210) still tells us nothing and is still excluded. We simply stopped discarding the games that do carry signal.

Why b fell

With more direct evidence the model trusts the ranking less. The prior weight b dropped from 1.24 → 0.86, and the count of teams with no data fell from nine to five. Predictions became noticeably less extreme — the most lopsided games stopped topping out near 90%.

04 — Feeding it real tournaments

Knockout football,
added to the mix

Qualifiers are useful but lopsided and low-stakes. The richest recent evidence about the contenders sits in the continental finals — so we added four of them, under the same in-scope rule: Euro 2024, Copa América 2024, and the Africa Cup of Nations 2023 (played Jan 2024) and 2025 (played Dec 2025) editions. Eighty-nine more in-scope games.

This was the unlock for the teams the model had been guessing at. Algeria, Egypt and Ivory Coast — three of the five remaining data-less sides — are AFCON regulars; Ivory Coast won the 2023 edition on home soil. Panama had played a full Copa América. Suddenly they all had real games. Only New Zealand, the lone Oceania qualifier whose opponents sit outside the top 50, was left rated by ranking alone.

And the payoff landed where it mattered most. On 261 games the in-sample calibration hit exactly 5.0% of results outside the 95% interval — the textbook target, and the very number Gelman's corrected 2014 model reported. The training set had finally grown into the model.

05 — The draw problem, and a second model

Modelling goals,
not gaps

The Student-t model's one structural weakness was draws. A continuous distribution sliced at ±0.5 goals can only ever approximate the most common result in the sport. So we built a second model designed for it.

The Dixon-Coles double-Poisson model — the textbook answer, and the very fix flagged in the original's caveats — throws out the score difference and models the two scorelines directly. Each team gets an attack and a defence rating; home and away goals are independent Poisson draws, with a correction term τ that nudges probability into the low-scoring results (0–0, 1–0, 0–1, 1–1) where real football clusters.

The dependence parameter ρ came out negative (≈ −0.04), which is exactly what lifts the draw probability for evenly-matched sides. Brazil vs Morocco, a near-coin-flip, went from a 28% draw under the threshold to 31% under Dixon-Coles — and the model now emits a most-likely scoreline for every fixture, not just W/D/L.

Two more things came for free. Modelling each scoreline let us add a real home-advantage term (worth about a 1.2× scoring boost), and separating attack from defence means a 3–2 win no longer looks identical to a 1–0. Same teams, same prior, same 261 games — a sharper lens.

06 — Simulating the whole World Cup

From one match
to one trophy

Once a model emits scorelines, it can play the whole tournament. We ran 10,000 simulated World Cups. Each one draws every group game from the Dixon-Coles distribution, builds all twelve tables under FIFA's real tie-breakers — points → goal difference → goals scored → head-to-head → drawing of lots — then takes the top two of each group plus the eight best third-placed teams into the new 32-team knockout.

The knockout runs on the official 2026 bracket, including the genuinely fiddly part: FIFA's allocation table that decides which group winner faces which qualifying third. Each tie is resolved as a single match, with a draw going to a coin-flip shootout, all the way to the final.

Two checks confirm the machinery: across the simulations exactly 32 teams reach the knockout on average, and the championship probabilities sum to one. The result is a full distribution over outcomes — every team's odds of reaching the Round of 32, and of lifting the trophy.

07 — Playing the leaderboard

The honest answer
is the optimal one

The predictions feed a ranked prediction game: you enter percentages for each fixture, and as results come in you're scored. The rule looked arbitrary — until the maths revealed it was designed to reward telling the truth.

For each fixture you enter (p₁, p₌, p₂). When the result is known, you score 100 minus a quadratic penalty on the two percentages you assigned to the outcomes that didn't happen. A perfect 100% call scores 100; backing a single wrong outcome at 100% scores 0.

The interesting question for a model-builder: what should you enter? We checked by brute force — grid-searching every integer entry for the one that maximises expected score — and the answer, on every fixture, is the model's own probabilities. That quadratic cross-term xy is precisely what makes the rule strictly proper: honest reporting is optimal. No cleverness required — paste the model's numbers in.

That also lets us value strategies. If the model is right, an honest slate expects ~5,113 points across the 72 games. Going all-in on every favourite throws away ~1,250 of those expected points in exchange for triple the variance — a gamble that only makes sense when you're trailing and need the swing.

The scoring rule — strictly proper
x, y = the two wrong outcomes' %

S = 100 − (x² + y² + xy) / 100

argmaxₙ E[S] = the true probabilities
→ just enter the model's numbers

08 — Reflections & what's next

What moved
the needle

Across all of it, two changes mattered most — and neither was a cleverer algorithm. Data quality and quantity took calibration from rough to textbook. And matching the model to the question — goals, not gaps — fixed draws and unlocked the whole tournament simulation.

The arc is tidy: a 110-game model that leaned on rankings and shrugged at draws became a 261-game, two-model system that is well-calibrated, simulates the tournament end to end, and provably knows the best way to enter its own predictions. The same skeleton throughout — Gelman's hierarchical idea, FIFA rankings as a prior, Stan doing the sampling — just fed better and pointed at the right targets.

Where it could go next

  • 1
    Temporal decay. A 2022 result still counts as much as a 2026 one. Dixon-Coles' original paper down-weights older games exponentially — a one-line change to the likelihood that would let current form speak louder.
  • 2
    An independent prior. FIFA rankings are computed from the same results we train on, so the prior and the data partly double-count. Swapping in club-Elo-derived ratings would break that overlap.
  • 3
    Squad-level features. The model is blind to injuries, call-ups and form of individual players. Squad-average club ratings would be the most ambitious — and probably the most rewarding — extension of all.
