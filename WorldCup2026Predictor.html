01 — Background

From a 2014 blog post
to 48 teams

In July 2014, statistician Andrew Gelman published a pair of blog posts on Statistical Modeling, Causal Inference, and Social Statistics in which he fitted a simple Bayesian hierarchical model to the ongoing FIFA World Cup in Brazil. The goal was modest: use the scores of completed group-stage games to estimate each team's relative ability, then generate probabilistic predictions for the remaining fixtures.

The model was elegant. Score differences between teams were modelled as draws from a Student-t distribution centred on the difference in latent team abilities. Each team's ability was in turn drawn from a normal distribution informed by FiveThirtyEight's Soccer Power Index — a prior that encoded pre-tournament knowledge about team strength. The non-centred parameterisation kept the sampler efficient. Stan did the rest.

"The key insight is that each game is an observation of the difference in abilities, not of the abilities themselves. The prior pulls everything toward the rankings; the data updates away from it."

The original implementation contained a subtle bug — the predictive intervals were miscalibrated — which Gelman corrected in a follow-up post. The corrected model showed that roughly 5% of actual score differences fell outside the 95% posterior predictive intervals: good calibration.

This project adapts Gelman's model for the 2026 World Cup, which is considerably larger (48 teams, 12 groups, 72 group games) and takes place over eleven years after the original. FIFA rankings replace the Soccer Power Index as the prior, and the training data combines the 2022 World Cup results with 2026 qualifying matches between qualified teams.

02 — Approach

Design decisions

Prior: FIFA Rankings

Gelman's original model used FiveThirtyEight's Soccer Power Index, treating it as a rank-ordered prior on team ability. We substitute the official FIFA World Rankings as of April 2025 — the most recent update before the tournament — which serves an equivalent function.

The raw rank numbers (1 = strongest) are inverted so that a higher numerical value corresponds to a stronger team, then rescaled to the interval [−1, 1] using Gelman's two-standard-deviation standardisation. The rescaling preserves the ordinal structure while putting the prior on the same scale as the likelihood.

One acknowledged limitation is that FIFA rankings are themselves computed from recent international results, some of which overlap with the qualifying matches used as training data. This means the prior and the likelihood are not fully independent — there is a modest double-counting of signal. For the purposes of this exercise we accept this and note it as a caveat.

Output: win / draw / loss

Gelman's model produces a continuous posterior predictive distribution over score differences, not discrete outcomes. To convert to win/draw/loss probabilities, we sample from the posterior predictive and apply a ±0.5 goal threshold:

  • Score diff > +0.5 → Team 1 wins
  • −0.5 ≤ Score diff ≤ +0.5 → Draw
  • Score diff < −0.5 → Team 2 wins

This approximation understates true draw frequency (football has a systematic excess of 0–0 and 1–1 draws versus what a continuous distribution predicts), but it is simple, transparent, and ensures the three probabilities always sum to exactly 100%.

Gelman convention

The model always treats the stronger team (lower FIFA rank number) as Team 1, regardless of fixture order. This convention ensures that the sign of the score difference is consistently interpretable: positive means the favourite won, negative means the upset. Probabilities are then reported back in fixture order.

Data filtering

The qualifying games are filtered to pairs where both teams are among the 32 48 qualified nations. This avoids the minnow problem: a 7–0 win by Germany over a non-qualifying nation would artificially inflate Germany's estimated ability. By only including matched qualified pairs, every observation is informative about the relative abilities of teams we actually care about.

OFC qualifying was excluded on the basis that Oceania produced only a single qualifier (New Zealand) via an intercontinental play-off, with no competitive group-stage matches between multiple qualified teams.

Neutral venue assumption

Home advantage is not modelled. World Cup group games are played at neutral venues, so no correction is needed there. Qualifying games do have a home team, and ignoring this introduces a modest bias — but the effect on ability estimates is diluted by the relatively small number of qualifying games per team in the dataset.

03 — Model

The statistical model

Likelihood

For each game k, let yk be the score difference (goals by the favourite minus goals by the underdog). The likelihood is a Student-t distribution with fixed degrees of freedom ν = 7:

Equation 1 — Likelihood
yk ∼ t7( ai(k) − aj(k), σy )
// for each game k, fave i vs. underdog j

The Student-t with ν = 7 has heavier tails than a normal distribution, accommodating the occasional dramatic scoreline (e.g. 7–1) without treating it as an impossibility. The degrees of freedom are fixed rather than estimated — a deliberate choice following Gelman's original to keep the model simple and identifiable.

Prior on team ability

Each team's latent ability ai is modelled as drawn from a normal distribution whose mean is a linear function of the FIFA ranking prior score si:

Equation 2 — Ability prior
ai ∼ Normal( b ⋅ si, σa )
// s_i is the rescaled FIFA ranking score for team i

The parameter b captures how much weight to assign to the prior. If the FIFA rankings contain no information about true ability, the posterior of b should concentrate near zero. In practice the model found b ≈ 1.24, indicating the rankings are genuinely informative.

Non-centred parameterisation

To improve sampler efficiency — especially for teams with few or no observations, where the geometry of the posterior can be problematic — the model uses a non-centred form:

Equation 3 — Non-centred form (as implemented in Stan)
ãi ∼ Normal(0, 1)
ai = b ⋅ si + σa ⋅ ãi
// ã_i is the standardised latent ability

Posterior predictive sampling

For each fixture, win/draw/loss probabilities are computed by drawing posterior predictive samples of the score difference and classifying them according to the ±0.5 threshold. With 8,000 posterior samples, each probability is estimated to within roughly ±0.5 percentage points (Monte Carlo standard error).

Calibration

On the 110 training games, only 3.6% of actual score differences fell outside the 95% posterior predictive interval. A perfectly calibrated model would produce exactly 5%. This slight over-coverage suggests the model's intervals are marginally conservative — a safe direction for prediction.

Implementation

The model is implemented in Stan (via PyStan 3) and sampled using Hamiltonian Monte Carlo with the No-U-Turn Sampler (NUTS). Four independent chains of 2,000 samples each (after 1,000 warmup) were run, giving 8,000 effective posterior draws. No divergences or R-hat warnings were observed.

04 — Data

Training data & rankings

Match results

Match results were sourced from the martj42/international_results repository on GitHub — a continuously updated, community-maintained dataset of international football results from 1872 to the present. Two subsets were extracted:

2022 FIFA World Cup (Qatar): all 46 group-stage games where both participants are among the 48 qualified nations for 2026. The 2022 WC provides the only available data for many UEFA and CONCACAF teams that do not play each other in confederation qualifying.

2026 FIFA World Cup qualifying: games played after the 2022 WC final (18 December 2022), filtered to matches where both the home and away team are among the 48 qualified nations. This yields 64 games, concentrated in CONMEBOL (South America) and AFC (Asia), where qualified teams frequently play each other in round-robin qualifying groups.

Coverage gaps

Nine of the 48 qualified teams have zero games in the training set against other qualified opponents. Their ability estimates are driven entirely by the FIFA ranking prior:

Algeria • Cape Verde • Egypt • Ivory Coast • New Zealand • Norway • Panama • Scotland • South Africa

For these teams, predictions are essentially the prior — a reflection of their FIFA ranking relative to opponents. Treat these predictions with lower confidence than those for data-rich teams like Argentina (16 games), Brazil (13), or Uruguay (13).

FIFA Rankings (April 2025)

Rankings are inverted and rescaled using the formula:

Prior score rescaling
si = (max(r) − ri + 1 − x) / (2 ⋅ SD(x))

where ri is team i's FIFA rank. This places all teams on a [−1.5, +0.7] scale with the average qualified team at zero.

06 — Caveats

Known limitations

This model is a faithful adaptation of an eleven-year-old blog post applied to a significantly larger tournament. It makes several deliberate simplifications that any user of the predictions should understand.

  • 1 Draws are structurally underestimated. The ±0.5 threshold on a continuous Student-t distribution produces draw probabilities that are lower than empirical football rates (roughly 22–27%). Real football has a systematic excess of 0–0 and 1–1 results versus what pure continuous prediction implies. A bivariate Poisson model with a Dixon-Coles low-score correction would handle this more naturally.
  • 2 Nine teams are prior-only. Algeria, Cape Verde, Egypt, Ivory Coast, New Zealand, Norway, Panama, Scotland, and South Africa have no head-to-head games against other 2026 qualifiers in the training set. Their predictions are pure expressions of FIFA ranking, with no learning from match outcomes.
  • 3 Prior and likelihood partially overlap. FIFA rankings are computed from recent international results, some of which are also in the training set. This double-counting modestly over-informs the prior. An independent prior (such as club-ELO ratings) would be cleaner.
  • 4 Home advantage is not modelled. Qualifying games include a designated home team. Ignoring this introduces a small bias into ability estimates, though its effect is diluted by the limited number of qualifying games per team.
  • 5 No temporal decay. A qualifying result from September 2023 is given equal weight to one from March 2026. Applying an exponential decay function — down-weighting older matches — would allow the model to be more sensitive to recent form changes.
  • 6 Score difference, not goals. Modelling the difference rather than individual goal counts loses information about offensive and defensive quality separately, and loses the integer structure of scores. A Poisson or negative-binomial model on goals would be a natural next step.
